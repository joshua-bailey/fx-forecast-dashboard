Macro differentials

Methodology

Model selection based on rolling-window backtests (1993–2025). Real UIP is the only model that beats the random walk baseline across all horizons (1q, 2q, 4q). Carry (Fama) provides an alternative mechanism with good directional accuracy (~51%).

Uncovered Interest Parity (UIP)

UIP posits that the expected change in the exchange rate equals the interest rate differential between two countries: the high-yield currency is expected to depreciate. The forecast path compounds forward quarterly from the current spot rate using EIU policy rate forecasts.

For USD/JPY (foreign per USD):

E[S(t+k)] = S(t) × ∏ (1 + i_JP/4) / (1 + i_US/4)

For EUR/USD and GBP/USD (USD per foreign):

E[S(t+k)] = S(t) × ∏ (1 + i_US/4) / (1 + i_foreign/4)

where S(t) is the spot exchange rate, E[S(t+k)] is the expected rate k quarters ahead, i is the annualised policy rate (divided by 4 for quarterly compounding), and the product runs over each quarter in the forecast horizon.

Real Interest Rate Differential UIP

A variant of UIP that uses real interest rates (nominal rate minus CPI y/y inflation) rather than nominal rates. The intuition is that real return differentials, not nominal ones, drive international capital flows.

r_real = i_nominal − π_yoy

where r_real is the real interest rate, i_nominal is the nominal policy rate, and π_yoy is year-on-year CPI inflation. The real rate differential is then used in the same compounding formula as standard UIP.

Vector Error Correction Model (VECM)

An econometric model that captures both long-run equilibrium (cointegration) and short-run dynamics. Used by central banks (ECB, Fed) for structural FX analysis. The model assumes the exchange rate and its fundamentals share a common stochastic trend and mean-revert toward equilibrium.

Δy_t = αβ′y_(t-1) + ΓΔy_(t-1) + ε_t

where y = (log FX, rate differential, inflation differential, growth differential), α is the adjustment speed (how fast FX corrects to equilibrium), β is the cointegrating vector (long-run equilibrium relationship), and Γ captures short-run dynamics.

Market forward (CIP)

Outright forward rates derived from interbank forward quotes. Forward points are added to the current spot rate to give the rate that can be locked in today for settlement at each tenor (3M, 6M, 1Y, 2Y). By covered interest parity, the forward embeds the same rate differential as UIP but uses actual market deposit rates rather than policy rate forecasts — so differences between the two reflect the term premium and the market’s view on the rate path versus EIU’s.

Carry-Adjusted UIP (Fama β)

Fama (1984) showed that regressing exchange rate changes on interest rate differentials yields a coefficient β that is empirically negative — the opposite of what UIP predicts. With β = −1, high-yield currencies appreciate rather than depreciate (the carry trade). This layer uses additive quarterly compounding:

Δs = α + β(i − i*) + ε   [β = −1.0] S(t+k) = S(t) × ∏ (1 + β × diff / 4)

where Δs is the change in the log exchange rate, β is the Fama regression coefficient (set to −1), i and i* are domestic and foreign interest rates, diff is the annualised interest rate differential, and the product compounds quarterly. The carry path is the mirror image of standard UIP, reflecting the forward premium puzzle.
